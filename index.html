Installation Tutorials

Step-by-step guides for common plumbing installations

How to Install a Toilet

1Turn off water supply and drain existing toilet

2Remove old toilet (unscrew bolts, lift carefully)

3Clean the flange and inspect for damage

4Install new wax ring on flange (wax side down)

5Position new toilet bowl over bolts, press down firmly

6Install washers and nuts, tighten evenly (don't overtighten!)

7Attach tank to bowl with rubber gasket and bolts

8Connect water supply line to fill valve

9Turn on water, check for leaks, adjust float if needed

10Caulk around base (leave back open for leak detection)

Tip: Don't overtighten bolts - porcelain can crack! Use 3" or 4" drain pipe.

How to Install a Sink/Lavatory

1Measure and mark sink position on countertop/wall

2For drop-in: Cut hole using template, apply silicone

3Install faucet on sink before mounting (easier access)

4Set sink in place, secure with clips underneath

5Install P-trap (1.5" diameter for lavatory, 2" for kitchen)

6Connect trap to drain stub-out in wall

7Connect water supply lines (hot=left, cold=right)

8Use Teflon tape on threaded connections

9Turn on water slowly, check all connections for leaks

10Apply caulk around sink edge for waterproofing

Standard height: 32-36" from floor. Ensure proper slope on drain pipe (1/4" per foot).

How to Install PVC Pipes

1Measure and mark pipe cutting points accurately

2Cut pipe square using PVC cutter or hacksaw

3Deburr cut edges with deburring tool or sandpaper

4Dry-fit all pieces first to check alignment

5Clean pipe ends and fittings with PVC cleaner

6Apply PVC primer to both pipe and fitting

7Apply PVC cement evenly on both surfaces

8Insert pipe into fitting with 1/4 turn, hold 30 seconds

9Wipe excess cement immediately

10Let cure 15 minutes before handling, 2 hours before pressure

Work in ventilated area! PVC cement fumes are toxic. Wait 24 hours before full pressure test.

How to Install a Water Pump

1Choose proper location (ventilated, accessible, protected from rain)

2Install pump on concrete pad or sturdy platform

3Install foot valve at end of suction pipe in water source

4Connect suction pipe to pump inlet (use check valve)

5Connect discharge pipe to pump outlet

6Install pressure switch and pressure tank

7Wire pump to electrical supply (hire electrician if unsure)

8Prime pump by filling with water through priming port

9Turn on power, check for leaks and proper pressure

10Adjust pressure switch settings (typically 30-50 PSI)

Suction lift limit: 25 feet maximum. Use 1" or larger suction pipe. Ground pump electrically!

How to Install a Water Heater

Electric Water Heater:

1Turn off power at circuit breaker

2Install heater on sturdy floor or wall mount

3Install Temperature & Pressure Relief (TPR) valve

4Connect cold water inlet (usually marked blue)

5Connect hot water outlet (usually marked red)

6Install discharge pipe from TPR valve to floor drain

7Wire according to manufacturer specs (220V typical)

8Fill tank completely, open hot water tap to bleed air

9Turn on power, set thermostat (60-70¬∞C recommended)

10Check for leaks after 1 hour of operation

Gas Water Heater:

1Ensure proper ventilation and clearances

2Install vent pipe properly (must slope upward)

3Connect gas line using approved black iron or CSST pipe

4Use pipe thread compound (not Teflon on gas lines)

5Test for gas leaks with soap solution

6Light pilot according to manufacturer instructions

Gas installation requires licensed professional! LPG leaks are extremely dangerous. Always test connections.

How to Install Faucets & Fixtures

1Turn off water supply valves

2Remove old faucet if replacing

3Clean mounting surface thoroughly

4Install rubber gasket or apply plumber's putty to base

5Feed supply lines through mounting holes

6Secure faucet from underneath with washers and nuts

7Connect supply lines to shutoff valves

8Use Teflon tape on threaded connections (3-4 wraps clockwise)

9Hand-tighten first, then 1/4 turn with wrench

10Turn on water slowly, check for leaks

11Run water for 2-3 minutes to flush debris

Common types: Compression, ball, cartridge, ceramic disk. Know your type for repairs!

General Installation Tips

  • Always turn off water/power first - Safety is priority!
  • Dry-fit everything - Check alignment before permanent installation
  • Use proper tools - Right tool prevents damage and ensures quality
  • Don't overtighten - Can crack fixtures or strip threads
  • Use Teflon tape correctly - Clockwise, 3-4 wraps, not on compression fittings
  • Test for leaks - Run water for several minutes after installation
  • Follow local codes - Check municipality requirements
  • Keep work area clean - Prevents contamination and accidents
  • Take photos - Document before/during installation for reference
  • Have spare parts - Extra washers, fittings save time

Repair & Troubleshooting Guides

Common plumbing problems and how to fix them

How to Fix Leaking Pipes

Detection Methods:

  • Visual inspection - Look for water stains, drips, puddles
  • Listen for hissing sounds in walls or ceiling
  • Check water meter - If running when all fixtures off, leak exists
  • Use moisture meter for hidden leaks

Temporary Fixes:

1Turn off water supply immediately

2Dry the pipe thoroughly

3Apply pipe repair tape/epoxy putty (temporary)

4Use pipe clamp for pinhole leaks

Permanent Solutions:

1Cut out damaged section of pipe

2Measure and cut replacement pipe

3Install couplings or fittings on both ends

4For threaded pipes: Apply thread compound, tighten

5For PVC: Clean, prime, cement properly

6For copper: Solder joints carefully

7Test for leaks at low pressure first

Leaks near electrical should be handled immediately! Risk of electrocution. Call professional if needed.

How to Unclog Drains

Method 1: Plunger (For minor clogs)

1Block overflow opening with wet cloth

2Fill sink/tub with 3-4 inches of water

3Place plunger over drain completely

4Plunge vigorously 15-20 times

5Repeat 3-4 times if necessary

Method 2: Drain Snake/Auger (For stubborn clogs)

1Remove drain cover/stopper

2Insert snake into drain opening

3Turn handle clockwise while pushing forward

4When you hit clog, push and rotate to break it up

5Pull out snake slowly, clean debris

6Flush with hot water

Method 3: Chemical Drain Cleaner (Use sparingly)

1Remove standing water if possible

2Pour recommended amount (read label!)

3Wait specified time (usually 15-30 minutes)

4Flush with hot water

Method 4: P-Trap Cleaning (Most effective)

1Place bucket under P-trap

2Loosen slip nuts with pliers

3Remove P-trap carefully

4Clean out debris and buildup

5Reassemble, ensure proper seal

6Test for leaks

Never mix drain cleaners! Can create toxic fumes. Wear gloves and eye protection.

How to Fix Running Toilets

Diagnosis:

  • Remove tank lid, identify which part is faulty
  • Add food coloring to tank - if color appears in bowl, flapper leaks
  • Check if water continuously flows into overflow tube

Fix 1: Adjust Float (Water level too high)

1Turn off water supply

2Flush to empty tank

3Adjust float arm or adjustment screw

4Water should be 1" below overflow tube

5Turn water back on and test

Fix 2: Replace Flapper (Leaking seal)

1Turn off water, flush toilet

2Remove old flapper from flush valve

3Take old flapper to store for exact match

4Install new flapper, connect chain

5Adjust chain - 1/2" slack when closed

6Turn water on, test flush

Fix 3: Replace Fill Valve (Continuous running)

1Turn off water, flush tank

2Disconnect water supply line

3Remove old fill valve (locknut underneath)

4Install new fill valve, adjust height

5Reconnect water supply

6Adjust float to proper level

A running toilet wastes 200+ gallons per day! Fix immediately to save water and money.

How to Replace Faucet Washers

For Dripping Faucets:

1Turn off water supply valves

2Close drain or cover with cloth (prevent losing parts)

3Remove handle (unscrew or pry off cap, then screw)

4Remove packing nut with wrench

5Pull out stem/cartridge

6Remove old washer from bottom of stem

7Install new washer (exact same size)

8Replace O-ring on stem if worn

9Reassemble in reverse order

10Turn water on slowly, test for leaks

Washer Sizes (Common Philippine sizes):

  • Lavatory faucet: 1/2" or 3/8" washers
  • Kitchen faucet: 1/2" to 5/8" washers
  • Shower/bath: 5/8" to 3/4" washers
Bring old washer to hardware store for exact match. Wrong size won't seal properly!

How to Fix Low Water Pressure

Diagnosis Steps:

1Determine if problem is localized or whole house

2Check if hot or cold water affected (or both)

3Ask neighbors if they have same issue

Solution 1: Clean Aerator (Single fixture affected)

1Unscrew aerator from faucet spout

2Disassemble parts carefully (note order)

3Soak in vinegar for 30 minutes

4Scrub screen with old toothbrush

5Rinse and reassemble

Solution 2: Check Main Shutoff Valve

1Locate main water shutoff valve

2Ensure it's fully open (turn counterclockwise)

3Check individual fixture shutoff valves too

Solution 3: Check Pressure Regulator

1Locate pressure regulator (near main shutoff)

2Adjust screw clockwise to increase pressure

3Normal range: 40-60 PSI

4Use pressure gauge to measure

Solution 4: Clear Pipe Blockages

1Check for mineral buildup in pipes

2May require pipe replacement if severe

3Consider water softener for hard water areas

Solution 5: Pump Issues (For pump systems)

1Check pump power supply

2Inspect pressure switch settings

3Check pressure tank (may need recharging)

4Examine foot valve in well/source

If whole neighborhood affected, contact water district. Don't adjust beyond recommended pressure!

Troubleshooting Quick Reference

  • Gurgling Drains: Vent pipe blocked - check roof vent for debris/bird nests
  • Water Hammer (banging pipes): Install water hammer arrestor or air chamber
  • Sewer Smell: Dry P-trap - pour water down unused drains monthly
  • Slow Draining: Partial clog or improper slope - snake drain or check pipe slope
  • Hot Water Runs Out Fast: Sediment in heater - flush tank annually
  • Frozen Pipes: Insulate pipes in cold areas, leave faucets dripping in freezing weather
  • Sweating Pipes: High humidity - insulate cold water pipes
  • Rusty Water: Corroded pipes or water heater - may need replacement

When to Call a Professional

  • Gas line leaks (smell of gas - evacuate immediately!)
  • Main sewer line blockages
  • Water heater replacement
  • Extensive pipe replacement
  • Permit-required work
  • Underground pipe repairs
  • Complex pump problems
  • When you're unsure - better safe than sorry!
Emergency contacts: Keep your local plumber's number handy. For gas leaks, call fire department immediately!

System Design Tutorials

Learn to design efficient and code-compliant plumbing systems

How to Design a Water Supply System

Step 1: Determine Water Demand

1Count all fixtures (toilets, sinks, showers, etc.)

2Assign fixture units (FU) to each:

  • Water closet (toilet): 3 FU
  • Lavatory (sink): 1 FU
  • Bathtub/Shower: 2 FU
  • Kitchen sink: 2 FU
  • Washing machine: 2 FU
  • Urinal: 3 FU

3Total all fixture units

4Calculate peak demand using Hunter Curve

Step 2: Size the Main Service Line

  • 1-12 FU = 3/4" pipe
  • 13-40 FU = 1" pipe
  • 41-80 FU = 1-1/4" pipe
  • 81-150 FU = 1-1/2" pipe
  • 151-300 FU = 2" pipe

Step 3: Design Distribution System

1Plan shortest routes to all fixtures

2Use trunk-and-branch or loop system

3Branch lines typically 1/2" for fixtures

4Main distribution: 3/4" to 1" diameter

5Install shutoff valves at strategic points

Step 4: Calculate Pressure Requirements

  • Minimum static pressure: 20 PSI at highest fixture
  • Recommended: 40-60 PSI
  • Maximum: 80 PSI (use pressure reducer if higher)
  • Pressure loss: 0.5 PSI per foot of elevation
  • Friction loss: Calculate for pipe length and diameter

Step 5: Plan Storage & Pumping

1Calculate daily water consumption (100-150 L/person/day)

2Size storage tank: 1-2 days supply minimum

3Position tank at highest practical point

4If pump needed, calculate Total Dynamic Head (TDH)

5Select pump based on flow rate and TDH

Always include expansion/reserve capacity of 20-30% for future needs!

How to Design a Drainage System

Step 1: Understand Drainage Components

  • Drainage pipes: Carry waste water away
  • Traps: Prevent sewer gas entry (P-trap, S-trap)
  • Vents: Allow air circulation, prevent siphoning
  • Cleanouts: Access points for maintenance

Step 2: Size Drain Pipes

Based on Drainage Fixture Units (DFU):

  • Toilet: 4 DFU (minimum 3" drain)
  • Lavatory: 1 DFU (1.5" drain)
  • Kitchen sink: 2 DFU (2" drain)
  • Bathtub: 2 DFU (1.5" drain)
  • Shower: 2 DFU (2" drain)
  • Floor drain: 2 DFU (2" drain)

Branch Drain Sizing:

  • 1-1/2" pipe: up to 3 DFU
  • 2" pipe: up to 6 DFU
  • 3" pipe: up to 20 DFU
  • 4" pipe: up to 160 DFU

Step 3: Calculate Proper Slope

1Minimum slope requirements:

  • Pipes 2.5" or less: 1/4" per foot (2%)
  • Pipes 3" to 6": 1/8" per foot (1%)
  • Pipes larger than 6": 1/16" per foot (0.5%)

2Never exceed 45-degree angle changes

3Use long-sweep fittings for smoother flow

Step 4: Design Venting System

1Every fixture needs venting (direct or indirect)

2Vent sizing:

  • Single fixture: minimum 1-1/4" vent
  • Multiple fixtures: 1-1/2" to 2" vent
  • Main stack vent: same size as largest drain (minimum 3")

3Vent pipe must rise above flood level rim

4Terminate 6" above roof (12" in areas with heavy snow)

5Keep vents 10 feet from windows/doors/air intakes

Step 5: Plan Cleanout Locations

1Install at base of each vertical stack

2Every change of direction over 45 degrees

3Maximum 50 feet between cleanouts on horizontal runs

4Make accessible (not inside walls without access panel)

Critical: All drains must flow downhill! Use level to verify slope during installation.

How to Calculate Pipe Sizes

Water Supply Pipe Sizing Formula:

Use the Hazen-Williams equation for friction loss:

  • Q = Flow rate (GPM)
  • D = Pipe diameter (inches)
  • L = Pipe length (feet)
  • H = Head loss (PSI)

Quick Reference - Max Flow Rates:

  • 1/2" pipe: 5 GPM (19 LPM)
  • 3/4" pipe: 12 GPM (45 LPM)
  • 1" pipe: 25 GPM (95 LPM)
  • 1-1/4" pipe: 40 GPM (150 LPM)
  • 1-1/2" pipe: 60 GPM (230 LPM)
  • 2" pipe: 100 GPM (380 LPM)

Velocity Limits:

  • Cold water: Maximum 8 feet per second
  • Hot water: Maximum 5 feet per second (prevents water hammer)
  • Recommended: 3-5 feet per second for quiet operation

Drainage Pipe Capacity:

At 1/4" per foot slope:

  • 1-1/2" drain: 8 GPM
  • 2" drain: 21 GPM
  • 3" drain: 42 GPM
  • 4" drain: 180 GPM
  • 6" drain: 625 GPM

Practical Calculation Steps:

1Determine total fixture units

2Calculate probable simultaneous use (not all fixtures on at once)

3Find required flow rate from charts

4Select pipe diameter that handles flow with acceptable pressure loss

5Verify velocity is within limits

Oversizing pipes wastes money. Undersizing causes poor performance. Calculate carefully!

Design Best Practices

  • Keep it simple: Fewer fittings = less friction loss and fewer leak points
  • Group fixtures: Minimize pipe runs by placing bathrooms/kitchens back-to-back
  • Accessibility: Design for easy maintenance and future repairs
  • Allow expansion: Include couplings and unions for future modifications
  • Protect pipes: Insulate hot water lines, protect from freezing
  • Consider noise: Use sound-dampening hangers, avoid sharp bends
  • Future-proof: Oversize main lines slightly for additions
  • Document everything: Create as-built drawings for future reference

Septic Tank Maintenance

Understanding Your Septic System:

  • Solid waste settles at bottom (sludge)
  • Grease floats at top (scum layer)
  • Liquid wastewater flows to drain field
  • Bacteria break down organic matter

Inspection Schedule:

1Inspect tank every 6 months

2Check sludge and scum levels

3Pump when sludge reaches 1/3 tank capacity

4Typical pumping: every 2-3 years for household

Pumping Procedure:

1Locate and expose septic tank lid

2Call licensed septic pumping service

3Have both inlet and outlet chambers pumped

4Inspect tank for cracks or damage

5Check baffles for deterioration

6Keep records of pumping dates

What NOT to Flush:

  • ‚ùå Grease, fats, cooking oil
  • ‚ùå Coffee grounds, eggshells
  • ‚ùå Paper towels, napkins
  • ‚ùå Sanitary napkins, diapers
  • ‚ùå Cigarette butts
  • ‚ùå Cat litter
  • ‚ùå Chemicals, paint, pesticides
  • ‚ùå Pharmaceuticals

Drain Field Care:

  • Never drive or park vehicles over drain field
  • Don't plant trees near drain field (roots damage pipes)
  • Divert roof drains and surface water away
  • Keep grass mowed, avoid deep-rooted plants
Signs of failure: Standing water, sewage odor, slow drains, gurgling sounds. Call professional immediately!

Water Tank Cleaning

Why Clean Water Tanks?

  • Sediment accumulation affects water quality
  • Bacteria and algae growth health hazard
  • Reduces storage capacity over time
  • Prevents contamination and waterborne diseases

Cleaning Frequency:

  • Overhead tanks: Every 3-6 months
  • Underground tanks: Every 6-12 months
  • More frequent in rainy season or if water source is dirty

Cleaning Procedure:

1Turn off water supply to tank

2Close outlet valve to prevent draining

3Use stored water or drain to safe location

4Open tank access hatch/manhole

5Remove all sediment and debris manually

6Scrub walls and floor with stiff brush

7Use mild chlorine solution (1:10 ratio) for disinfection

8Let solution sit for 30 minutes

9Rinse thoroughly with clean water multiple times

10Drain completely, let dry if possible

11Inspect tank for cracks, leaks, or damage

12Check and clean inlet strainer/filter

13Seal access hatch properly

14Refill tank and discard first batch of water

Safety Precautions:

  • Wear rubber boots, gloves, and mask
  • Ensure adequate ventilation (enclosed spaces dangerous!)
  • Never enter tank alone - have assistant outside
  • Use proper ladder or safety harness
  • Don't use harsh chemicals or detergents
Never use soap or detergents in water tanks! Use only approved food-grade disinfectants.

2Inspect electrical connections (electric heaters)

3Check gas burner and pilot light (gas heaters)

4Test thermostat accuracy

5Inspect vent pipe for blockages (gas heaters)

  • Install timer for off-peak heating

Pipe Pressure Testing Procedure

Why Pressure Test?

  • Verify system integrity before concealment
  • Detect leaks early (cheaper to fix now!)
  • Required by building code and inspectors
  • Prevents water damage and callbacks

Water Pressure Test (For Water Supply Lines):

1Complete all installations and connections

2Cap all open outlets securely

3Fill system completely with water, bleed all air

4Connect pressure gauge at convenient test point

5Pressurize to 150 PSI (or 1.5x working pressure)

6Hold pressure for 2 hours minimum

7Inspect all joints, connections, and pipes

8Pressure drop should not exceed 5 PSI

9Mark any leaks, repair, and retest

10Document results (take photos for records)

Air Pressure Test (Alternative Method):

1Cap all openings securely

2Connect air compressor with pressure regulator

3Pressurize slowly to 50-60 PSI

4Apply soap solution to all joints

5Look for bubbles indicating leaks

6Hold pressure for 15 minutes minimum

7No pressure drop indicates leak-free system

Drainage System Test:

1Plug all drain outlets except highest one

2Fill system with water to highest fixture

3Let stand for 15 minutes

4Water level should not drop

5Check for leaks at all joints

CRITICAL: Never exceed rated pressure! Over-pressurizing can burst pipes and cause injury.

How to Install PEX Piping

What is PEX?

  • Cross-linked Polyethylene - flexible plastic tubing
  • Increasingly popular in modern plumbing
  • Easier to install than rigid pipes
  • Resistant to corrosion and scale buildup
  • Good for both hot and cold water

Types of PEX:

  • PEX-A: Most flexible, best for cold climates
  • PEX-B: Most common, good balance of properties
  • PEX-C: Least expensive, stiffer than A or B

Color Coding:

  • Red: Hot water lines
  • Blue: Cold water lines
  • White: Can be used for either (mark appropriately)

Crimp Method Installation:

1Cut PEX tubing square with PEX cutter

2Slide copper crimp ring onto tubing (1" from end)

3Insert brass fitting into tubing (push fully)

4Slide crimp ring to 1/4" from end of tubing

5Position crimp tool over ring

6Squeeze handles completely until they release

7Use go/no-go gauge to verify proper crimp

8If gauge doesn't fit, crimp is good

Expansion Method Installation:

1Cut PEX tubing square

2Insert expansion tool into tubing end

3Expand tubing to larger diameter

4Quickly insert PEX expansion fitting while expanded

5Tubing shrinks back, creating watertight seal

6Let connection sit 30 seconds to contract

Clamp (Cinch) Method:

1Similar to crimp, but uses stainless steel clamps

2Uses different tool (cinch clamp tool)

3Clamp tightens with screw mechanism

4Verify tab is bent over completely

Installation Best Practices:

  • Support every 32" for horizontal runs
  • Protect from UV light (degrades PEX over time)
  • Keep away from hot surfaces (furnaces, water heaters)
  • Allow for expansion/contraction in long runs
  • Don't use threaded fittings directly on PEX
  • Use manifold system for easier shutoff of individual lines
  • Test at 150 PSI for 2 hours before concealment
Always use correct tool for your connection type! Crimp tools won't work on expansion fittings and vice versa.

How to Thread Galvanized Pipes

When to Thread Pipes:

  • Galvanized iron (GI) pipe connections
  • Black iron gas pipe installations
  • Older homes requiring pipe extensions
  • Commercial applications requiring steel pipe

Tools Required:

  • Pipe threading machine or manual die set
  • Pipe cutter or hacksaw
  • Reamer/deburring tool
  • Pipe vise
  • Cutting oil
  • Thread compound or tape
  • Pipe wrenches (2 minimum)

Manual Threading Steps:

1Measure and mark pipe to length needed

2Add extra length for threads (3/4" for fittings)

3Secure pipe in vise

4Cut pipe square using pipe cutter

5Ream inside edge to remove burrs

6Apply cutting oil generously to pipe end

7Select correct die size for pipe diameter

8Position die square on pipe end

9Turn die handle clockwise (2 full turns)

10Back off 1/4 turn to break chips

11Continue: 2 turns forward, 1/4 turn back

12Add cutting oil frequently during process

13Thread until 2-3 threads visible past die

14Back die off completely, remove

15Clean threads with wire brush

16Test fit with coupling/fitting

Machine Threading:

1Much faster than manual threading

2Insert pipe into machine chuck

3Tighten chuck securely

4Select correct die head size

5Apply cutting oil to die head

6Start machine, engage die

7Machine automatically cuts threads

8Die reverses when proper depth reached

9Clean and inspect threads

Thread Quality Check:

  • Threads should be sharp and clean
  • No torn or ragged edges
  • Coupling should thread on by hand for 2-3 turns
  • Then require wrench to tighten
  • 2-3 threads should remain visible when tight

Assembly Tips:

  • Apply pipe thread compound (never Teflon tape on gas lines)
  • Thread compound on male threads only
  • Hand-tighten first, then use wrenches
  • Use two wrenches (one to hold, one to turn)
  • Don't overtighten - causes cracking
  • 1.5 to 2 full turns past hand-tight typically sufficient
Never use damaged or worn dies! They produce poor threads that will leak. Replace dies regularly for quality work.

How to Sweat Copper Pipes

What is "Sweating" Copper?

Soldering copper pipe joints - called "sweating" because flux bubbles and "sweats" out during heating.

Materials Needed:

  • Copper pipe and fittings
  • Propane or MAPP gas torch
  • Lead-free solder (required for potable water)
  • Flux (paste or liquid)
  • Emery cloth or sandpaper (120 grit)
  • Flux brush
  • Tubing cutter
  • Wire brush
  • Heat-resistant cloth or mat

Step-by-Step Soldering:

1Cut pipe square using tubing cutter

2Remove internal burr with reaming tool

3Clean pipe end with emery cloth until shiny (1" length)

4Clean inside of fitting with wire brush until shiny

5Apply thin layer of flux to pipe end

6Apply flux inside fitting

7Insert pipe into fitting completely (bottomed out)

8Wipe excess flux from joint

9Light torch, adjust to proper flame

10Heat fitting (not pipe) evenly around joint

11Move flame continuously to heat all sides

12Touch solder to joint (not flame) after 10-15 seconds

13When solder melts and flows, feed 1/2" to 3/4" length

14Solder should be "sucked" into joint by capillary action

15Remove heat immediately when joint is full

16Let cool naturally - DO NOT move or quench!

17When cool, wipe joint clean

18Inspect for complete solder ring around joint

Proper Flame Technique:

  • Blue inner cone indicates proper temperature
  • Keep flame tip about 1" from fitting
  • Heat fitting more than pipe
  • For vertical joints, heat from bottom up
  • Large fittings need more heating time

Common Soldering Mistakes:

  • ‚ùå Not cleaning thoroughly - causes poor bond
  • ‚ùå Too much flux - creates corrosion over time
  • ‚ùå Overheating - flux burns off, solder won't flow
  • ‚ùå Underheating - solder balls up, doesn't penetrate
  • ‚ùå Moving joint before cool - breaks bond
  • ‚ùå Using too much solder - wastes material
  • ‚ùå Heating solder directly - should melt from pipe heat

Soldering Vertical Joints:

  • Solder flows downward with gravity
  • Heat bottom of fitting first
  • Apply solder at top of joint
  • Solder flows down into joint gap
  • Turn fitting to solder all around

Working with Existing Water:

1Drain pipes completely before soldering

2Open faucets to relieve pressure

3If water still present, use bread trick:

  • Stuff white bread into pipe (absorbs water)
  • Solder joint normally
  • Bread dissolves when water turned back on
  • Clears out through faucet aerator
CRITICAL: Use only lead-free solder for drinking water! Lead solder is toxic and illegal for potable water systems.

How to Work with CPVC

What is CPVC?

  • Chlorinated Polyvinyl Chloride
  • Cream/beige colored rigid plastic pipe
  • Rated for hot water (up to 93¬∞C/200¬∞F)
  • More expensive than PVC but handles hot water
  • Common for residential water supply lines

CPVC vs. PVC:

  • CPVC: Hot/cold water supply (cream color)
  • PVC: Cold water and drainage only (white color)
  • Never use PVC for hot water - will soften and fail
  • Use correct cement for each type

Cutting CPVC:

1Measure and mark cut line

2Use ratchet-type plastic pipe cutter (cleanest cut)

3Or use fine-tooth saw (hacksaw with 32 teeth/inch)

4Cut must be square (90 degrees)

5Deburr inside and outside edges

6Remove any rough spots with sandpaper

Solvent Welding (Gluing) CPVC:

1Dry-fit all pieces first to verify alignment

2Mark depth of fitting on pipe for reference

3Mark alignment if orientation matters

4Clean pipe end and fitting socket with CPVC cleaner

5Let cleaner evaporate (5-10 seconds)

6Apply CPVC primer to pipe end (not PVC primer!)

7Apply primer inside fitting socket

8While primer is wet, apply CPVC cement

9Apply even coat to pipe end (not too thick)

10Apply cement inside fitting socket

11Immediately insert pipe into fitting

12Push to depth mark with 1/4 turn twist

13Hold in place 30 seconds (critical!)

14Wipe excess cement immediately

15Let cure 15 minutes before handling

16Wait 2 hours before running cold water

17Wait 24 hours before running hot water

Temperature Considerations:

  • Don't work in direct sunlight (cement sets too fast)
  • Ideal temperature: 60-100¬∞F (15-38¬∞C)
  • Cold weather: Allow longer cure times
  • Hot weather: Work quickly, cement sets faster
  • Keep cement cans closed when not using

Support Requirements:

  • Horizontal runs: Support every 3 feet
  • Vertical runs: Support every 5 feet
  • Use proper hangers (allow for expansion)
  • Don't over-tighten metal clamps on plastic pipe
  • Allow for thermal expansion in long runs

Common CPVC Pipe Sizes:

  • 1/2": Branch lines to fixtures
  • 3/4": Main distribution lines
  • 1": Supply to multiple bathrooms or floors
  • Uses same size fittings as copper equivalents
NEVER use PVC cement on CPVC! Use only CPVC-specific cement. Wrong cement will cause joint failure and leaks.

How to Repair Cast Iron Pipes

About Cast Iron Pipes:

  • Common in older homes (pre-1980s)
  • Very durable but eventually corrodes
  • Heavy and difficult to work with
  • Usually drainage/vent pipes (not water supply)
  • Lifespan: 50-100 years

Common Problems:

  • Rust and corrosion (especially at joints)
  • Cracking from age and movement
  • Deterioration of oakum/lead joints
  • Tree root intrusion

Temporary Repair - Pipe Wrap Method:

1Clean area around leak thoroughly

2Dry surface completely

3Apply epoxy putty over leak

4Smooth putty and let cure per instructions

5Or use rubber repair sleeve/coupling

6Position over damaged area

7Tighten clamps evenly on both sides

Permanent Repair - Banded Coupling Method:

1Identify section requiring replacement

2Support pipe on both sides of repair area

3Cut out damaged section with reciprocating saw

4Use metal-cutting blade

5Make cuts square to pipe

6Clean cut ends completely

7Measure gap, cut PVC/ABS replacement pipe

8Use no-hub coupling (rubber with metal bands)

9Slide couplings onto existing cast iron ends

10Insert replacement PVC pipe section

11Position couplings over joints

12Tighten band clamps with torque wrench

13Torque to manufacturer specs (typically 60 in-lbs)

14Test system for leaks

Working with No-Hub Couplings:

  • Also called "band couplings" or "Fernco couplings"
  • Rubber sleeve with stainless steel clamps
  • Allows connecting cast iron to PVC/ABS
  • Much easier than traditional lead and oakum
  • Requires no special tools or torch
  • Accommodates slight misalignment

Full Pipe Replacement:

When to replace entire section:

  • Multiple leaks in same pipe run
  • Extensive corrosion throughout
  • Cracking along length of pipe
  • During major renovation
  • If cost of repairs approaches replacement cost

Safety Precautions:

  • Cast iron is VERY heavy - get help for support
  • Wear safety glasses - metal shards when cutting
  • Use proper supports - pipe can shift when cut
  • Check for asbestos wrap on old pipes
  • May need professional for asbestos removal
Cast iron cutting creates hazardous dust! Always wear respirator and protect eyes when cutting or grinding cast iron.

How to Install a Water Softener

What is a Water Softener?

  • Removes calcium and magnesium (hard water minerals)
  • Uses ion exchange process with salt/resin
  • Prevents scale buildup in pipes and appliances
  • Improves soap efficiency
  • Extends appliance life (water heaters, washing machines)

When You Need a Water Softener:

  • White scale deposits on fixtures
  • Soap doesn't lather well
  • Clothes feel stiff after washing
  • Water test shows hardness over 7 grains/gallon (120 mg/L)
  • Frequent water heater element failure
  • Common in areas with well water or hard municipal supply

Sizing the Water Softener:

1Determine water hardness (test kit or water district)

2Calculate daily water usage (people √ó 100 gallons/day)

3Multiply: hardness √ó daily gallons = grains to remove daily

4Select softener capacity (multiply daily grains √ó 7 days minimum)

Example: 4 people, 15 grains hardness

  • 400 gallons/day √ó 15 grains = 6,000 grains/day
  • 6,000 √ó 7 days = 42,000 grain capacity needed

Installation Steps:

1Choose location near main water line entry

  • Protected from freezing and direct sun
  • Near drain for regeneration discharge
  • Near electrical outlet (110V)
  • Level floor, adequate space for maintenance

2Turn off main water supply

3Drain system pressure

4Install bypass valve assembly on main line

5Cut main water line where softener will connect

6Install inlet and outlet connections

7Connect softener to bypass valve (inlet from supply, outlet to house)

8Install drain line from softener to floor drain or standpipe

  • Minimum 1.5" drain line
  • Air gap required (2√ó drain line diameter)
  • Maximum 30 feet to drain

9Run overflow line to drain (gravity flow)

10Connect brine tank to softener control valve

11Add salt to brine tank (fill 2/3 full)

12Plug in power supply

13Turn on water slowly, check for leaks

14Program control valve (hardness, capacity, regeneration time)

15Initiate manual regeneration cycle

16Monitor first cycle, verify proper operation

Plumbing Considerations:

  • Don't soften outdoor hose bibbs (wastes salt, not needed)
  • Some prefer unsoftened cold to kitchen (cooking, drinking)
  • Install before water heater for maximum benefit
  • Bypass valve allows softener isolation for maintenance

Maintenance:

  • Keep brine tank at least 1/3 full of salt
  • Check salt level monthly
  • Clean brine tank annually
  • Replace resin bed every 10-15 years
  • Test water hardness quarterly to verify operation
Water softeners discharge salt brine during regeneration. Ensure drain can handle discharge and complies with local regulations.

How to Maintain a Deep Well

Common in Philippine Provinces:

Many homes rely on deep wells for water supply. Proper maintenance ensures clean, safe water and system longevity.

Well Components:

  • Well casing: Pipe extending into ground
  • Well cap: Seals top, prevents contamination
  • Submersible pump: Lifts water from depth
  • Pressure tank: Maintains system pressure
  • Pressure switch: Controls pump operation
  • Check valve: Prevents backflow

Annual Inspection Checklist:

1Inspect well cap and seal

  • Cap should be tight, no cracks
  • Screen vents clear of debris
  • No visible contamination entry points

2Check well casing above ground

  • No cracks, corrosion, or damage
  • Extends at least 12 inches above grade
  • Ground slopes away from casing

3Test water quality

  • Bacterial contamination (coliform test)
  • pH level (should be 6.5-8.5)
  • Hardness, iron, manganese levels
  • Test annually or after flooding/contamination

4Inspect pressure tank

  • Check for leaks, corrosion
  • Verify air pressure (should be 2 PSI below cut-in pressure)
  • Drain and flush if needed

5Test pressure switch operation

  • Verify proper cut-in/cut-out pressures (30/50 PSI typical)
  • Clean contacts if pitted

Well Disinfection (Chlorination):

Perform after:

  • New well installation
  • Pump replacement or repairs
  • Positive bacterial test
  • Flooding or contamination
  • Every 1-2 years as maintenance

Chlorination Procedure:

1Calculate chlorine needed (2 cups bleach per 100 feet of water column)

2Remove well cap

3Pour chlorine bleach down well casing

4Connect hose from outdoor faucet back to well

5Recirculate water for 30 minutes (mixes chlorine)

6Run each fixture until chlorine smell detected

7Let chlorine sit in system 12-24 hours

8Flush entire system until no chlorine smell

9Run outside hose to avoid chlorine in septic system

10Wait 1 week, then test water

Pump Maintenance:

  • Monitor for unusual noises or vibrations
  • Check amp draw annually (increasing = wearing pump)
  • Typical submersible pump life: 15-25 years
  • Keep records of cycling frequency
  • Short cycling indicates pressure tank issue

Common Well Problems:

  • Low water pressure: Check pressure switch, tank, pump
  • Cloudy water: Sediment, bacteria, or air - test and treat
  • Rusty water: Iron bacteria or pipe corrosion
  • Pump runs constantly: Leak, bad check valve, low water level
  • No water: Power issue, tripped breaker, failed pump
  • Sulfur smell: Bacteria in well - chlorinate and treat

Well Protection:

  • Keep 50+ feet from septic systems, animal pens
  • Don't use well for pesticide mixing or washing equipment
  • Maintain sanitary seal on well cap
  • Prevent surface water entry
  • Don't store chemicals near wellhead
  • After flooding, disinfect before using water

When to Call Professional:

  • Pump replacement (requires specialized equipment)
  • Well rehabilitation or deepening
  • Persistent contamination issues
  • Dramatic drop in water level
  • Casing damage or collapse
Never enter a well! Confined space, drowning, toxic gas hazards. Only licensed well drillers should access well interior.

Common Water Quality Issues (Philippines)

Issue 1: High Iron Content

Signs: Rusty/orange stains, metallic taste, reddish water

Solutions:

  • Water softener (if under 5 ppm)
  • Iron filter with oxidizing media
  • Chlorine injection + filtration
  • Reverse osmosis at point of use

Issue 2: Hard Water

Signs: Scale buildup, soap scum, spotted dishes, stiff laundry

Solutions:

  • Ion exchange water softener (most effective)
  • Salt-free conditioners (doesn't remove minerals)
  • Reverse osmosis for drinking water only

Issue 3: Bacterial Contamination

Signs: Positive coliform test, illness after drinking

Solutions:

  • UV sterilization system (kills bacteria)
  • Chlorination system (continuous or shock)
  • Ozone treatment
  • Well disinfection
  • Fix source of contamination

Issue 4: Sediment/Turbidity

Signs: Cloudy water, sand/particles, clogged aerators

Solutions:

  • Sediment filter (5-50 micron)
  • Multi-stage filtration
  • Well rehabilitation if source is well
  • Settling tank for high sediment

Issue 5: Low pH (Acidic Water)

Signs: Blue-green stains, pinhole leaks in copper, metallic taste

Solutions:

  • Calcite neutralizer (raises pH)
  • Soda ash injection
  • Blend with alkaline water

Issue 6: Chlorine/Chemical Taste

Signs: Strong chlorine smell/taste (municipal water)

Solutions:

  • Activated carbon filter (most common)
  • Reverse osmosis system
  • Catalytic carbon for chloramines

Issue 7: Sulfur/Rotten Egg Smell

Signs: Hydrogen sulfide odor, especially hot water

Solutions:

  • Chlorination + carbon filtration
  • Aeration system (releases gas)
  • Replace water heater anode rod
  • Hydrogen peroxide injection

Issue 8: High TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)

Signs: Salty taste, white residue, affects plant watering

Solutions:

  • Reverse osmosis (most effective)
  • Distillation
  • Deionization for specific applications

Water Testing Guide

When to Test Water:

  • New home or well
  • Annually for well water
  • After flooding or natural disasters
  • Change in taste, odor, or appearance
  • Illness suspected from water
  • Before installing treatment system
  • Pregnant women or infants in home

Basic Water Test Parameters:

  • Coliform bacteria: Indicates contamination
  • pH: Should be 6.5-8.5
  • Hardness: Calcium/magnesium content
  • Iron: Should be under 0.3 ppm
  • Manganese: Should be under 0.05 ppm
  • TDS: Total dissolved solids
  • Nitrates: Should be under 10 ppm (important for babies)

Where to Test in Philippines:

  • Department of Health (DOH) regional offices
  • Local Water District laboratories
  • Philippine General Hospital (PGH) laboratories
  • Private environmental testing labs
  • University laboratories (some offer services)
  • Home test kits (basic parameters only)

2Choose cold water tap (not filtered)

3Remove aerator from faucet

4Let water run 2-3 minutes

5Don't touch inside of bottle or cap

6Fill bottle to shoulder (leave air space)

7Cap tightly

8Keep cool, deliver to lab within 24 hours

Interpreting Results:

  • Consult with water treatment professional for solutions
  • Consider multiple treatment methods for multiple issues
  • Retest after installing treatment system

  • Materials: Only approved materials (PVC, CPVC, copper, GI pipes)
  • Backflow Prevention: Required for water supply protection

Common Pipe Sizes (Philippine Standard)

  • Water Service Line: 3/4" to 1" diameter
  • Branch Lines: 1/2" diameter
  • Toilet Drain: 3" to 4" diameter
  • Sink/Lavatory Drain: 1.5" to 2" diameter
  • Main Sewer Line: 4" to 6" diameter
  • Vent Pipes: 1.5" to 2" diameter

Safety Standards

  • Proper PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) required
  • Gas line installations must meet LPG safety standards
  • Pressure testing of water lines before concealment
  • Proper trenching and excavation practices
  • Compliance with fire safety codes
Always verify current codes with local building officials as regulations may vary by municipality and may be updated periodically.
